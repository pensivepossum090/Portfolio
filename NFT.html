NFTScoring Design

Designing a mobile application that allows users to make rapid and intelligent NFT trading decisions

Project Problem and Context

Context

I designed and developed an interface for the YCombinator startup NFTScoring with a small group for a UX class project. We intentionally did not look at NFTScoring’s existing website or products and based our design solely off the startup’s stated goals: "NFTScoring is the place for you to discover, analyze and trade NFTs. We give you the superpowers to understand the NFT market in any given moment, make the best decisions, and take faster actions."

Timeline: 3 Weeks (October - November 2022)

Team Size: 4

Role: Group member (design)

Design Problem

Current NFT marketplaces fail to incorporate comprehensive analysis into their products, and resultantly, users do not have the necessary tools and information to make rapid and intelligent NFT trading decision decisions.

Design Solution:

We designed a mobile application with an NFT marketplace, arecommendation system, and key analytics on each NFT.

Iterative Process

Even while time was severely constrained due to the need to both design and develop the product in 10 weeks, I still was able to use an iterative approach to the design, as shown below:

  1. 1) Product sketching
  2. 2) Feedback on sketches
  3. 3) Hi-Fi Mockups based on feedback
  4. 4) User feedback on mockups
  5. 5) Development of initial product (visible here)
  6. 6) User testing and feedback from community partners at UMiami
  7. 7) Development of final product

Design Choices and Iterations

Page Design

(original)

(final)

Stage: (2) Feedback on sketches

Change: Made the map take up the entire page instead of being just a section.

Rationale and Alternatives: People critiquing the sketches vastly preferred a design where the map took up the entire screen instead of just a small section of it. This makes sense as using the map is the main purpose of the site, so any extra information is extraneous.

Information Display

(original)

(final)

Stage: (2) Feedback on sketches

Change: Added an information display that pops up when a user clicks on or searches for a tract

Rationale and Alternatives: Someone critiquing the sketches suggested that more information being displayed when a user clicks on a specific neighborhood. This aligns with one of the main purposes of the map to allow users to see conditions in their neighborhood.

Map Legend

(original)

(final)

Stage: (2) Feedback on sketches

Change: Made the map legend continuous rather than discrete.

Rationale and Alternatives: A few people critiquing the sketches suggested that continuity of the data would be a more compelling representation. Later in the design process, when developing a hi-fi mockup it become apparent that this was a better approach.

Sidebar Location

(original)

(final)

Stage: (4) Feedback on mockups

Change: Moved the side panel from the left side of the screen to the right.

Rationale and Alternatives: Users interacting with the hi-fi mockup of the product preferred the panel on the left side of the screen to the right. One user articulated that they were more used to map panels being on the left, like with Google Maps. This suggestion made sense as most people that have used interactive maps have a mental model of a panel on the left side of the map.

Sidebar Design

(original)

(final)

Stage: (4) Feedback on mockups

Change: Switched the style of the side panel to a white background with blue, green, and black text.

Rationale and Alternatives: Users hated the blue color scheme and other dark color schemes a lot. When shown many different potential color schemes the white one performed the best by far.

Map Design

(original)

(final)

Stage: (4) Feedback on mockups

Change: Used a light map style instead of a dark one

Rationale and Alternatives: When shown a light and dark version of the map, all people prefered the light version, as with the sidebar.

Toggle Button Design

(original)

(final)

Stage: (6) User testing and feedback from community partners at UMiami

Change: Added affordances to layer toggle buttons including a blue outline on selection and a gray outline and cursor switch to a pointer on hover.

Rationale and Alternatives: In user testing it became obvious that the simple bold and normal button styles did not make it clear to users that they could switch between layers. The final design includes affordances to make the functionality more intuitive.

Popup Style

(original)

(final)

Stage: (6) User testing and feedback from community partners at UMiami

Change: Added more information to the popup including census tract and county information.

Rationale and Alternatives: Community partners suggested including more location-specific information on the popups. Feedback made sense as this served on of the primary goals of the map to allow users to explore factors in their specific neighborhood.

Outline Style

(original)

(final)

Stage: (6) User testing and feedback from community partners at UMiami

Change: Switch outline from black to white and black.

Rationale and Alternatives: In the first release of the map, it was sometimes difficult to see the outline when it was one tracts with darker colors. This adjustment makes it visible on tracts of all colors.

Point Style

(original)

(final)

Stage: (6) User testing and feedback from community partners at UMiami

Change: Removed labels from points and changed colors

Rationale and Alternatives: Users complained when testing that the text on the points often made the screen unnecessarily crowded. Switching to a simpler design addresses this issue.

Reflection

Humility is essential. In this project I found my own design assumptions to be constantly undermined in critiques and by users in product testing. Listening to users and advisors allowed this project to develop positively, a lesson that I will apply to the rest of my design work.

Additionally, throughout this project, I was forced to explore the tension between development and design and think about my own biases. As this was an independent project that I had to design and develop, I had to ensure that my own ideas about what would be feasible or easy to develop wouldn’t constrain the creation of the design. From this project I learned the importance in compartmentalizing these elements to ensure the best product is ultimately built.
